# Narrative Report Templates for Higher Education Economic Analysis

Structures and writing guidelines for creating compelling stakeholder communications that integrate FRED data.

## Report Types

### Executive Brief (1-2 pages)
**Audience**: Trustees, Board, President, Cabinet
**Focus**: Strategic implications, high-level trends
**Data depth**: Summary metrics with 1-2 visualizations
**Tone**: Confident, forward-looking, action-oriented

### Analytical Report (5-10 pages)
**Audience**: VPs, Deans, Directors
**Focus**: Detailed trends, comparative analysis
**Data depth**: Multiple visualizations, statistical analysis
**Tone**: Balanced, evidence-based, nuanced

### Stakeholder Communication (2-4 pages)
**Audience**: Faculty, staff, external partners
**Focus**: Context and interpretation
**Data depth**: Key visualizations with accessible explanations
**Tone**: Informative, transparent, engaging

### Student/Parent Facing (1-2 pages)
**Audience**: Prospective students, parents
**Focus**: ROI story, career outcomes
**Data depth**: Clear comparisons, relatable metrics
**Tone**: Encouraging, realistic, empowering

```markdown
# [Compelling Title]

## Executive Summary
[2-3 paragraphs]
- Opening statement of core finding
- Key data point supporting the finding
- Strategic implication for the institution

## The Economic Context
[Section showing overall landscape]

- Bachelor's unemployment: X.X% vs X.X% for HS-only
- Earnings premium: +XX%

[Visualization: Trend chart of primary metric]

### Historical Trends
[5-10 year perspective with recession context]
- How patterns have evolved
- Stability through economic cycles
- Implications of trajectory

[Visualization: Multi-year comparison]

## The Employment Advantage
[Analysis of unemployment differentials]

### Labor Market Protection
- Bachelor's unemployment consistently ~X% vs ~X% for HS
- Gap widens during recessions (protective effect)
- Recovery patterns by education level

[Visualization: Unemployment rates by education over time]

### Key Insights
[Insight box]
- What the data reveals about higher ed's stabilizing effect
- Implications for student messaging
- Connection to institutional mission

## The Earnings Differential
[Analysis of income advantages]

### Lifetime Value Proposition
- Weekly earnings: $X,XXX (Bachelor's+) vs $XXX (HS)
- Annual differential: ~$XX,XXX
- Lifetime projection: $XXX,XXX+ advantage

[Visualization: Earnings comparison over time]

### ROI Perspective
- Average debt load context: $XX,XXX
- Payback timeline at differential: X-X years
- Net lifetime benefit: $XXX,XXX+

[Visualization: Bar chart showing earnings premium]

### Key Insights
[Insight box]
- ROI remains compelling despite debt concerns
- How to frame this in student communications
- Supporting evidence for financial aid policies

## Strategic Implications

### For Student Recruitment
- Value proposition messaging
- Addressing debt concerns with data
- Differentiating from competitors

### For Retention Efforts
- Supporting students through to completion
- Financial stress intervention strategies
- Long-term perspective counseling

### For Financial Aid Policy
- Manageable debt thresholds
- Grant vs. loan strategy
- Communications framework

### For External Relations
- Board/trustee talking points
- Public advocacy positions
- Media response guidance

## Limitations and Context
[Critical section - always include]

### What This Data Shows
- National averages and trends
- Aggregate patterns across all fields

### What This Data Doesn't Show
- Individual variation by field of study
- Specific institutional outcomes
- Non-monetary value of education
- Selection effects (degree holders may differ in unmeasured ways)

### Recommendations for Interpretation
- Use as context, not prediction
- Supplement with institution-specific data when available
- Recognize field-of-study variation
- Acknowledge that past performance doesn't guarantee future results

## Data Sources
[Always cite specifically]

- Student Loan Debt: FRED Series SLOAS (Student Loans Owned and Securitized)
- Unemployment Rates: FRED Series LNS14027662 (Bachelor's+), LNS14027660 (HS)
- Earnings Data: FRED Series LEU0252918500A (Bachelor's+), LEU0252917300A (HS)
- All data via Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis FRED API
- Retrieved: [Date]

## Conclusion
[2-3 paragraphs]
- Restate core finding
- Connect to institutional strategic priorities
- Call to action or recommended next steps
```

## Writing Guidelines

### Opening Hooks
**Strong**: "Despite headlines about student debt, bachelor's degree holders earn 75% more than high school graduates while experiencing unemployment rates less than half those of their non-degree-holding peers."

**Weak**: "This report examines student loan data and employment outcomes by educational attainment level."

**Why**: Lead with the finding, not the method. Hook readers with the story the data tells.

### Data Integration Patterns

**Show, then interpret**:
```markdown
Bachelor's degree holders earned a median of $1,432 per week in 2023, compared to $853 for high school graduates—a 68% premium. Over a career spanning 40+ years, this differential translates to more than $1.2 million in additional lifetime earnings.
```

**Context before numbers**:
```markdown
Higher education serves as a powerful economic stabilizer. During the 2020 recession, bachelor's degree holders saw unemployment peak at 8.4%, while those with only high school diplomas experienced rates exceeding 15%.
```

**Comparative framing**:
```markdown
The earnings advantage of a bachelor's degree has remained remarkably consistent. Since 2010, degree holders have earned 65-80% more than high school graduates, demonstrating durable value across economic cycles.
```

### Stakeholder-Specific Language

**For Trustees/Board**:
- "Strategic positioning"

- "Value proposition"
- "Market differentiation"
- "Long-term sustainability"

**For Academic Affairs**:
- "Student success metrics"
- "Completion outcomes"
- "Degree value"
- "Career preparation"
- "Educational quality indicators"

**For Enrollment Management**:
- "Recruitment messaging"
- "Value narrative"
- "Yield strategies"
- "Family decision-making"
- "Competitive positioning"

**For Financial Aid**:
- "Manageable debt levels"
- "Net price context"
- "ROI framework"
- "Affordability strategies"
- "Access and success balance"

### Tone Calibration

**Evidence-based confidence**: "The data clearly demonstrates that degree holders experience substantially lower unemployment rates."

**Not overconfident**: "The data suggests strong correlation between degree attainment and employment outcomes."

**Balanced**: "While individual experiences vary significantly, aggregate data shows consistent patterns of advantage for degree holders."

**Not defensive**: "Despite critics' claims about higher education's value, the data shows..."

**Action-oriented**: "This data enables institutions to craft evidence-based recruitment messages that resonate with price-sensitive families."

**Not merely descriptive**: "This data shows that bachelor's degree holders earn more than high school graduates."

## Visualization Integration

### Effective Chart + Narrative Patterns

**Pattern 1: Build anticipation**
```markdown
The unemployment differential between degree holders and high school graduates tells a compelling story about higher education's protective effect.

[Chart: Unemployment rates by education level, 2010-2024]

The visualization reveals that bachelor's degree holders consistently experience unemployment rates roughly half those of high school graduates. This differential is not merely statistical—it represents thousands of individuals whose degree credentials provided labor market resilience during economic turbulence.
```

**Pattern 2: Insight before visual**
```markdown
Perhaps most striking is the consistency of the earnings advantage over time.

[Chart: Earnings premium trend]

As the chart demonstrates, the bachelor's earnings premium has hovered between 65-80% for more than a decade, resisting both recessionary pressures and technological disruption. This stability suggests the value proposition is structural, not cyclical.
```

**Pattern 3: Visual as evidence**
```markdown
Critics sometimes suggest that student debt has outpaced the value of degrees. However, when we examine the full economic picture:

[Combined chart: Debt levels vs. earnings differential]

The lifetime earnings advantage ($1.2M+) substantially exceeds typical debt loads ($30K average), creating a compelling ROI even accounting for interest costs.
```

### Chart Caption Guidelines

**Good caption**: "Unemployment rates by educational attainment, 2015-2024. Bachelor's degree holders (green) consistently experience rates roughly half those of high school graduates (orange). Gap widens during recessions, demonstrating higher education's stabilizing effect."

**Weak caption**: "Chart showing unemployment data by education level from FRED database, series LNS14027662 and LNS14027660."

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

### Don't: Present debt in isolation
"Student loan debt has reached $1.77 trillion."
**Problem**: Creates alarm without context.

### Do: Provide ROI framing
"While student debt totals $1.77 trillion nationally, bachelor's degree holders earn $600,000+ more over their careers than high school graduates, creating a substantial net benefit even after debt repayment."

### Don't: Claim causation
"Getting a degree causes higher earnings."
**Problem**: Correlation vs. causation confusion.

### Do: Acknowledge complexity
"Bachelor's degree holders earn substantially more, though this correlation reflects both the direct value of credentials and potential differences in degree seekers' characteristics."

### Don't: Cherry-pick timeframes
"From 2019-2020, unemployment increased 150% for degree holders."
**Problem**: COVID is an outlier; misleading without context.

### Do: Show patterns over time
"While COVID temporarily disrupted employment patterns, bachelor's degree holders have consistently experienced unemployment rates 50-60% below those of high school graduates over the past 15 years."

### Don't: Ignore field variation
"All college degrees provide strong ROI."
**Problem**: Overgeneralization ignores significant field differences.

### Do: Acknowledge nuance
"While aggregate data shows strong ROI for bachelor's degrees, outcomes vary significantly by field of study. Engineering and healthcare programs typically show faster payback periods than humanities or social sciences."

## Templates by Use Case

### Template: Board Report on Higher Ed Value
**Length**: 2 pages
**Sections**: Executive Summary, Key Findings (debt, employment, earnings), Strategic Implications, Data Sources
**Visualizations**: 2 (unemployment comparison, earnings differential)
**Tone**: Confident, strategic
**Focus**: What this means for institutional positioning

### Template: Enrollment Messaging Brief
**Length**: 3 pages
**Sections**: The Value Story, Key Data Points, Message Testing, Recommended Talking Points
**Visualizations**: 3 (ROI comparison, unemployment protection, earnings trajectory)
**Tone**: Action-oriented, practical
**Focus**: How to communicate value to prospective students

### Template: Financial Aid Policy Memo
**Length**: 4 pages
**Sections**: Current Debt Context, Earnings Analysis, Manageable Debt Thresholds, Policy Recommendations
**Visualizations**: 2-3 (debt trends, earnings, ROI calculations)
**Tone**: Analytical, measured
**Focus**: Data-informed policy decisions

### Template: Public Advocacy One-Pager
**Length**: 1 page
**Sections**: Key Finding, Supporting Data, Implications for State/Region
**Visualizations**: 1 (most compelling metric)
**Tone**: Accessible, compelling
**Focus**: Quick read for policymakers or media

## Revision Checklist

Before finalizing any report:
- [ ] Does opening paragraph state the finding, not just the topic?
- [ ] Is every data point necessary to support the argument?
- [ ] Are limitations acknowledged without undermining the analysis?
- [ ] Is language appropriate for the target audience?
- [ ] Do visualizations enhance understanding rather than just decorating?
- [ ] Are all FRED series properly cited?
- [ ] Does conclusion include actionable implications?
- [ ] Is the tone balanced (not defensive, not overly promotional)?
- [ ] Have field-of-study variations been acknowledged?
- [ ] Is the ROI story told without presenting debt in isolation?
