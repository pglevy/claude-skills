# Stakeholder Personas for Higher Education Economic Communications

Audience-specific strategies for presenting FRED economic data to different institutional stakeholders.

## Persona 1: Board of Trustees / Governing Board

### Who They Are
- Fiduciary oversight responsibility
- Strategic decision-makers
- Often business or professional backgrounds
- Limited time for deep data dives
- Concerned with institutional reputation and financial sustainability

### What They Care About
- **Strategic positioning**: How does higher ed value affect competitiveness?
- **Risk management**: Are we positioned well if market sentiment shifts?
- **ROI narrative**: Can we defend tuition levels?
- **Enrollment trends**: Will families still choose us?
- **Public perception**: How do we communicate value externally?

### Communication Strategy

**Format Preference**: 2-page executive brief or 10-minute board presentation
**Data Depth**: High-level trends, key metrics only
**Visualization**: 1-2 impactful charts maximum
**Language**: Business-oriented, strategic

**Key Messages**:
1. Higher education continues to deliver strong ROI despite debt concerns
2. Our value proposition is supported by Federal Reserve economic data
3. This data provides foundation for strategic communications and positioning

**Do**:
- Lead with strategic implications
- Use business language (ROI, value proposition, competitive advantage)
- Connect data to board decisions (tuition, financial aid policy, marketing)
- Be confident but acknowledge complexity

**Don't**:
- Bury the lead in methodology
- Overwhelm with statistical detail
- Present data without strategic context
- Ignore legitimate concerns about debt

**Sample Framing**:
"Our institution's value proposition is grounded in economic reality. Federal Reserve data shows bachelor's degree holders earn 75% more while experiencing half the unemployment of high school graduates. This data enables evidence-based communications with prospective students and supports our strategic enrollment initiatives."

## Persona 2: President / Cabinet / C-Suite

### Who They Are
- Day-to-day institutional leadership
- Balance competing priorities
- Public-facing institutional voices
- Responsible for both internal and external communications
- Navigate between board expectations and campus realities

### What They Care About
- **Actionable intelligence**: How does this inform decisions?
- **Communication tools**: What can we say publicly?
- **Crisis preparedness**: How do we respond to debt crisis narratives?
- **Stakeholder alignment**: How do we keep different groups informed?
- **Resource allocation**: Where should we invest based on this data?

### Communication Strategy

**Format Preference**: 3-5 page analytical report with executive summary
**Data Depth**: Key findings + supporting detail for questions
**Visualization**: 3-4 charts covering main themes
**Language**: Professional, nuanced, actionable

**Key Messages**:
1. Data provides foundation for institutional messaging strategies
2. Evidence supports targeted investments in completion and affordability
3. Economic analysis equips leadership for media and policy conversations

**Do**:
- Provide both summary and detail (progressive disclosure)
- Include talking points they can use
- Anticipate questions they'll receive
- Offer cross-functional implications (enrollment, financial aid, academics)

**Don't**:
- Present only problems without solutions
- Ignore political/social dimensions
- Overstate certainty in predictions
- Miss opportunities to connect to strategic plan

**Sample Framing**:
"This analysis equips leadership with evidence-based talking points for multiple audiences. The data demonstrates that our degrees deliver measurable value while acknowledging the legitimate concerns families have about affordability. It supports strategic investments in both access and completion."

## Persona 3: Enrollment Management / Admissions

### Who They Are
- Front-line recruiters and yield strategists
- Directly engage prospective students and families
- Performance measured by enrollment numbers
- Navigate price sensitivity and value questions daily
- Need compelling, accessible messaging

### What They Care About
- **Recruitment messaging**: What can we tell families about ROI?
- **Competitive differentiation**: How do we stand out?
- **Objection handling**: How do we address debt concerns?
- **Yield strategies**: What convinces admitted students to enroll?
- **Counselor talking points**: How do we equip our team?

### Communication Strategy

**Format Preference**: Practical brief with messaging guide
**Data Depth**: Key stats that resonate with families + backup detail
**Visualization**: Student/parent-friendly graphics, shareable
**Language**: Accessible, reassuring, empowering

**Key Messages**:
1. Degrees deliver measurable career and earnings advantages
2. Investment in education yields long-term financial returns
3. Data shows employment protection during economic uncertainty

**Do**:
- Provide ready-to-use talking points
- Create family-friendly visualizations
- Address specific objections ("Is college worth it?")
- Include real-world translation ($X more per year = Y)

**Don't**:
- Use academic jargon
- Present data without human context
- Ignore emotional dimensions of decision
- Overpromise individual outcomes

**Sample Talking Points**:
- "On average, bachelor's degree holders earn $1,432 per week compared to $853 for high school graduates—that's over $30,000 more per year."
- "During economic downturns, degree holders experience roughly half the unemployment rate of those without degrees."
- "The lifetime earnings difference of $1.2 million substantially exceeds typical student debt loads."

**Sample Objection Responses**:

*"Is college really worth the debt?"*
"That's a great question. Let's look at the numbers. While the average bachelor's degree recipient borrows about $30,000, degree holders earn roughly $30,000 more per year than high school graduates. Over a career, that's more than $1.2 million in additional earnings. The math shows a strong return on investment."

*"What about stories of graduates who can't find jobs?"*
"Individual experiences vary, which is why choosing the right field and completing your degree matters so much. What the data shows is that overall, bachelor's degree holders have unemployment rates around 2-3%, roughly half the rate of those with only a high school diploma. Your degree provides significant labor market protection."

## Persona 4: Financial Aid Office

### Who They Are
- Balance access and affordability
- Counsel students on borrowing decisions
- Develop institutional aid policies
- Navigate federal regulations and institutional budgets
- See firsthand impact of debt on students

### What They Care About
- **Manageable debt levels**: What's reasonable to borrow?
- **ROI counseling**: How do we help students understand value?
- **Policy development**: What should our aid strategies be?
- **Student success**: Are we setting students up well?
- **Default prevention**: How do we minimize financial distress?

### Communication Strategy

**Format Preference**: Analytical memo with policy implications
**Data Depth**: Detailed for policy decisions, simplified for student counseling
**Visualization**: Debt-to-earnings ratios, payback timelines
**Language**: Balanced, realistic, supportive

**Key Messages**:
1. Data helps contextualize debt in relation to expected outcomes
2. Reasonable borrowing for degree completion yields strong returns
3. Evidence supports focusing on completion, not just access

**Do**:
- Provide debt-to-income guidelines grounded in data
- Show payback timelines at different earnings levels
- Connect to actual student advising scenarios
- Acknowledge field-of-study variation

**Don't**:
- Minimize legitimate debt concerns
- Present averages without acknowledging variation
- Ignore students who don't complete
- Make promises about individual outcomes

**Sample Counseling Framework**:
"Let's talk about the economics of your education. Federal data shows that in your intended field, typical earnings are $X per year, while you're planning to borrow $Y. That creates a manageable debt-to-income ratio. Here's what your monthly payments would look like and how that compares to the earnings advantage your degree provides..."

## Persona 5: Academic Affairs / Faculty

### Who They Are
- Focus on educational quality and student learning
- May be skeptical of economic framing
- Value intellectual and civic outcomes beyond earnings
- Concerned about commodification of education
- Play key role in retention through teaching and advising

### What They Care About
- **Holistic value**: How do we measure success beyond salary?
- **Student support**: How do we help struggling students persist?
- **Disciplinary variation**: What about fields with different economics?
- **Educational mission**: Are we staying true to our values?
- **Retention strategies**: How do we support completion?

### Communication Strategy

**Format Preference**: Contextual memo acknowledging non-monetary value
**Data Depth**: Supplement with engagement/civic participation data when possible
**Visualization**: Comparison across fields, not just overall averages
**Language**: Respectful of educational mission, not purely transactional

**Key Messages**:
1. Economic data provides context, not the complete picture
2. Understanding labor market realities helps us support students better
3. Completion matters tremendously for realizing educational benefits

**Do**:
- Acknowledge that education's value extends beyond earnings
- Frame economic data as one lens, not the only lens
- Connect to student success and completion initiatives
- Show discipline-specific variation when possible

**Don't**:
- Present education as purely economic transaction
- Ignore faculty concerns about commodification
- Suggest teaching should be driven by labor market alone
- Dismiss importance of liberal arts or non-vocational learning

**Sample Framing**:
"While education's value clearly extends beyond economics—critical thinking, civic engagement, personal growth—economic security matters to our students and their families. Understanding labor market realities helps us counsel students toward completion and supports our retention efforts. The data shows that finishing the degree dramatically improves outcomes, which aligns with our educational mission."

## Persona 6: External Relations / Communications

### Who They Are
- Manage institutional reputation
- Handle media inquiries
- Develop marketing messages
- Navigate public controversies
- Create content for diverse audiences

### What They Care About
- **Message testing**: What resonates with different audiences?
- **Media response**: How do we handle tough questions?
- **Public narrative**: How do we shape the conversation?
- **Evidence base**: What claims can we substantiate?
- **Crisis preparation**: How do we respond to debt crisis stories?

### Communication Strategy

**Format Preference**: Messaging guide with Q&A
**Data Depth**: Headline stats + detailed backup for tough questions
**Visualization**: Media-ready graphics, shareable on social
**Language**: Accessible, quotable, evidence-based

**Key Messages**:
1. Higher education delivers measurable economic value
2. Our institution is committed to both access and affordability
3. Data shows employment and earnings advantages are substantial and consistent

**Do**:
- Provide media-ready quotes and sound bites
- Prepare for skeptical questions
- Create shareable data visualizations
- Anticipate multiple audience segments

**Don't**:
- Overstate claims that data can't support
- Ignore legitimate debt concerns
- Present overly rosy picture
- Forget that different audiences need different messages

**Sample Media Talking Points**:

*"Why should students take on debt for college?"*
"The question isn't whether to avoid all debt, but whether the investment makes sense. Federal Reserve data shows bachelor's degree holders earn 75% more over their careers while experiencing half the unemployment. The average graduate borrows $30,000 but gains $1.2 million in additional lifetime earnings. That's a solid return on investment."

*"What about graduates who can't find work?"*
"Individual experiences vary, but the broader pattern is clear: bachelor's degree holders have unemployment rates around 2-3%, compared to 5-6% for high school graduates. Degree holders have substantially better labor market outcomes, even during recessions."

*"Hasn't student debt become a crisis?"*
"Student debt requires serious attention, which is why we're focused on both affordability and completion. The data shows that reasonable borrowing for degree completion yields strong returns—the lifetime earnings premium substantially exceeds typical debt. The real challenge is for students who borrow but don't complete. Our completion initiatives address this."

## Persona 7: Prospective Students & Parents

### Who They Are
- Making enrollment decision
- Concerned about cost and value
- May lack financial literacy
- Influenced by media narratives about debt
- Want best possible future for student

### What They Care About
- **"Will I get a job?"**
- **"Can I pay back loans?"**
- **"Is this worth it?"**
- **"Am I making a good decision?"**
- **"What's my likely salary?"**

### Communication Strategy

**Format Preference**: Clear, visual 1-pager or web content
**Data Depth**: Simple comparisons, relatable numbers
**Visualization**: Accessible charts, avoid complexity
**Language**: Reassuring, concrete, empowering

**Key Messages**:
1. College graduates get jobs—unemployment is half that of high school grads
2. You'll earn significantly more—about $30K more per year
3. The investment pays off—lifetime earnings exceed debt by over $1 million

**Do**:
- Use concrete numbers families can relate to ($X per week, $Y per year)
- Show comparison visuals (degree vs. no degree)
- Provide realistic timelines and expectations
- Acknowledge that completion is key

**Don't**:
- Use jargon or academic language
- Present overwhelming data
- Make it feel like "just numbers"
- Ignore their emotional concerns

**Sample Student-Facing Content**:

**"What will I earn?"**
Looking at the data: Bachelor's degree holders earn about $1,432 per week on average, compared to $853 for high school graduates. That's over $30,000 more per year, which adds up to more than $1.2 million over a career.

**"What about debt?"**
The average student borrows about $30,000. While that sounds like a lot, your degree gives you earning power to pay it back. With that $30,000+ annual earnings boost, most graduates can manage their monthly payments while still earning much more than they would without a degree.

**"What if I can't find a job?"**
Unemployment for bachelor's degree holders is around 2-3%, compared to 5-6% for people with just a high school diploma. Having a degree roughly cuts your unemployment risk in half. And during recessions, that protection becomes even more important.

## Cross-Persona Considerations

### Universal Principles Across All Audiences
1. **Evidence-based**: Ground all claims in FRED data
2. **Balanced**: Acknowledge limitations and variation
3. **Honest**: Don't overpromise or minimize legitimate concerns
4. **Actionable**: Connect data to decisions people need to make

### Adapting Data Complexity

**High complexity audiences** (Board, Cabinet, Faculty):
- Show methodology
- Acknowledge statistical nuances
- Present multiple data sources
- Discuss limitations extensively

**Medium complexity audiences** (Enrollment, Financial Aid, External Relations):
- Lead with findings
- Provide methodology on request
- Focus on practical applications
- Balance detail with accessibility

**Low complexity audiences** (Students, Parents, General Public):
- Simplify to core comparisons
- Use concrete examples
- Minimize methodology discussion
- Focus on "what this means for you"

### Timing and Context

**Proactive Communications** (not responding to crisis):
- Can be more nuanced and exploratory
- Time for education and context-building
- Opportunity to shape narrative

**Reactive Communications** (responding to debt crisis stories):
- Must be simpler and more direct
- Lead with reassurance
- Provide clear, quotable statistics
- Acknowledge concerns before countering

### Common Pitfalls to Avoid Across All Personas

1. **One-size-fits-all messaging**: Trustees need different communication than students
2. **Data without story**: Numbers alone don't persuade
3. **Ignoring emotional dimensions**: Decisions aren't purely rational
4. **Overconfidence**: Individual outcomes vary significantly
5. **Defensive tone**: Acknowledge concerns, don't dismiss them
6. **Jargon creep**: Match language to audience sophistication
7. **Missing the "so what"**: Always connect data to implications
